Why Arkisto
It is increasingly apparent that research projects need to provide for their endings before they begin. A project run out of the Social and Cultural Informatics Platform at the University of Melbourne is the New Life for Digital Research (NeLDiR) which is using Arkisto's methods to preserve research outputs. NeLDiR is inspired by existing work done at Kings College (London) on rescuing expired research projects, or the work of the Canadian Project Endings, both of which are leading the way.
A major driver for Arkisto are collections of research material that are no longer looked after. Some current examples are:
  • The eScholarship Research Centre, University of Melbourne. The ESRC has nearly 1,000 projects, mostly stored in MS Access databases, but with html versions generated from those databases. As the ESRC has been closed in 2020, the pressing issue is what to do to preserve all of this valuable scholarship.
  • Omeka repositories
  • FMPro catalogs, e.g. at the Grimwade Centre for Cultural Materials Conservation which catalogs hundreds of Indigenous artworks. Data stored in this kind of proprietary format needs to be exported into meaningful output and Arkisto addresses this need.
  • AIATSIS
